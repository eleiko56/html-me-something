My Writing Life: An Ongoing and Unpredictable Saga

An unplanned career

I never planned to be a writer. In fact, I'm still not entirely sure what it means to be a writer. When an American meets someone for the first time, usually one of the first questions out of his or her mouth is "What do you do?" and to say "I'm a writer" sounds both pretentious and delusional. To most people a writer is either someone whose novels perennially top the New York Times bestseller list, or a hobbyist who self-publishes novels about her cats.

Without getting all existential about it, I'm a person who has published multiple books with major publishers, mostly as an extension of my work in statistics, medicine, and public health. I had no idea when I submitted my first book proposal that writing would become such an important part of my life, but I'm really glad it has.

Besides books and professional articles, I do a lot of "utility" writing and editing as well. Did you ever wonder who writes test banks and instructor's manuals for textbooks? Or the entries in all those encyclopedias in the library? Probably someone like me. It's not glamorous, but it pays. This kind of writing is also useful practice in getting over yourself and getting on with the task at hand.

It all started with SPSS

I got a late start in the writing business, after spending quite a few years working as a statistician. My first book grew naturally out of that line of work, as I noticed there was no book on the market that discussed SPSS code clearly and simply, with a view to practical applications including data management tasks. At the time, most beginning SPSS books assumed the user was only interested in running the program through the GUI menus (better known as the "click and shit" approach) rather than learning the program's syntax. My opus 1 is not much of a beach read, but it's still in print, and is still a useful resource for those who work with SPSS.

Data analysis for people who hate to collect data

The second book also drew directly on the work I was doing as a statistician. To put it simply, I like analyzing data a whole lot more than I like collecting it. My interests also run to questions too large to be answered by the type of data a single research group can collect. The solution: work with secondary data, meaning data collected by someone else (often a governmental agency like the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. I was recruited to write this book by a representative of Cambridge University Press, which gives me a good name to drop when I want to impress someone.

A real money-spinner: My first book with O'Reilly

My next book caused me the most grief, but has also been the most rewarding. I wrote it to fill yet another unmet need--a statistics book for adults who need to learn some statistics for their work, or who took a course a long time ago and need a practical refresher. I get a bump in sales every August and September, so I suspect a lot of college students are buying it also. It was my first experience collaborating on a book with someone (via long distance--my co-author lives in Australia!), and was also a good lesson in the benefits of writing for a respected series (people don't necessarily know me, but they know the O'Reilly Nutshell series).

The gift that keeps on giving

There's now a second edition (with me as sole author) and some translations also! Authors often say that their dream is to be on the "permanent backlist", which means that your book remains in the publisher's active catalogue for years, and the royalties continue to roll in for years after you completed the book. Harper Lee is the model for this kind of success--you only need write one book in your life if it's as good, and as perennially popular, as To Kill a Mockingbird. I'm nowhere near her class, of course, but Statistics in a Nutshell is the kind of steady earner that makes it easier to ride out the feast-or-famine cycle typical of a freelance writer's income.

Breaking into the Editing Market

I got my start as an editor at about the same time I was writing Statistics in a Nutshell, and I'm particularly proud of the first encyclopedia I edited, the Encyclopedia of Epidemiology, published by SAGE. Editing an encyclopedia is a more influential job than you might think. The Editor-in-Chief gets to decide what articles will be included in the encyclopedia, for instance, and I included more biostatistical articles than a pure epidemiologist probably would have, as well as articles about public health issues like harm reduction and threats faced by sexual minorities.

Health Care: Other Choices are Available

Both personally and professional, I find the American "system" of health care to be bizarre, wasteful, cruel. Being a person who works with data, and is always open to hearing how someone else has solved a problem, I thought a book about how health care works in other countries might help people realize that other countries have found better ways to deliver care. Unfortunately, there's too many financial interests, and too much belief in American exceptionalism, to make information a particularly useful weapon in this battle.

Working with ABC-CLIO

I started writing for ABC-CLIO, a company specializing in reference books for public and undergraduate libraries, a few years ago. Writing for that audience demands clarity and particular attention to presenting controversial issues in a balanced manner. My specialties at ABC-CLIO are medicine and public health, and writing for them has allowed me to explore topics of interest to me personally that are also of national significance. My Transgender Health Issues>, my second book with ABC-CLIO, came out in 2018; I wrote a book about drug resistance for them, and am currently working on a volume about genetic testing.

A Reference Volume for Gale

One of the advantages of knowing statistics and being comfortable with secondary data is that you can write knowledgeably about subjects outside your specific professional focus. Case in point: a series volume I wrote for Gale about careers and occupations. My job on this volume was similar to that of an editor, since my main task was to update statistics in the previous volume (which were largely based on government reports issued every year or two. I also added some new sections (even worked in my favorite economist, Paul Krugman), and Gale gave me an author credit.

Plans for the future

There are so many books I want to write, but choosing which projects to pursue next isn't always a simple decision. The world needs a good guide to math for biology students and lab workers, for instance, and I have a model in mind, but have several other things to finish first. On a more popular note, I really enjoy the Quercus 'in minutes" series and it does not currently have a volume on statistics. I know I'll get that pitch out the door any day now...
